Abstract

Abstract

► Paper text — Abstract

We study how generative AI, and in particular agentic AI, shapes human learning incentives and the long-run evolution of society’s information ecosystem. We build a dynamic model of learning and decision-making in which successful decisions require combining shared, community-level general knowledge with individual-level, context-specific knowledge; these two inputs are complements.

Learning exhibits economies of scope: costly human effort jointly produces a private signal about their own context and a “thin” public signal that accumulates into the community’s stock of general knowledge, generating a learning externality. Agentic AI delivers context-specific recommendations that substitute for human effort. By contrast, a richer stock of general knowledge complements human effort by raising its marginal return.

The model highlights a sharp dynamic tension: while agentic AI can improve contemporaneous decision quality, it can also erode learning incentives that sustain long-run collective knowledge. When human effort is sufficiently elastic and agentic recommendations exceed an accuracy threshold, the economy can tip into a knowledge-collapse steady state in which general knowledge vanishes ultimately, despite high-quality personalized advice.

Welfare is generally non-monotone in agentic accuracy, implying an interior, welfare-maximizing level of agentic precision and motivating information-design regulations. In contrast, greater aggregation capacity for general knowledge — meaning more effective sharing and pooling of human-generated general knowledge — unambiguously raises welfare and increases resilience to knowledge collapse.

Generative AI — especially agentic AI that gives personalised, context-specific recommendations — can improve individual decisions in the short run while systematically destroying collective human knowledge in the long run.

The mechanism: when AI supplies good personalised advice, individuals have less incentive to learn. But human learning also produces general knowledge — the shared, community-level understanding that everyone draws on. As individuals learn less, this commons erodes. Once it falls below a critical threshold, the system tips into a knowledge-collapse steady state — zero collective knowledge, permanently.

Central finding: welfare is non-monotone in AI accuracy. There is an interior welfare-maximising level of AI precision, beyond which more accurate AI is harmful.

► CS translation · The sixty-second version

Stack Overflow is dying because everyone uses ChatGPT. This paper asks: what happens next, formally? Answer: if AI is accurate enough, and if human effort adjusts quickly enough to reduced incentives, the death of knowledge-sharing platforms is not a temporary transition — it’s a permanent equilibrium.

The model formalises exactly that dynamic, derives the conditions under which it’s inevitable, and characterises the regulatory policy that prevents it. Optimal fix: make AI deliberately less accurate than its maximum, and invest heavily in infrastructure for humans sharing what they know.

Section 1

Introduction

► Paper text — Introduction, part 1: optimists vs sceptics

Rapid advances in (generative) AI since 2022 have raised hopes of widespread benefits from superior AI-provided information provision and task services. AI models can now analyze and write text on an impressive range of topics on a par with specialized human experts and can sift through vast data sets to recognize patterns and locate relevant useful information. Most importantly, they can use these capabilities to provide context-specific information and recommendations. Nevertheless, growing use of generative AI tools has also raised fears about how reliance on AI will impact human cognition, knowledge and creativity.

Opinions are divergent on this question. On one side are commentators and experts who believe that with the use of AI tools human knowledge will expand greatly, for example, in the form of faster and better scientific discoveries, and much better decision-making by professionals with access to AI. In this spirit, Demis Hassabis has described AI as “the ultimate tool to help scientists, help us explore the universe around us.” At the other end of the spectrum are those concerned with the effects from growing reliance on AI for classroom work on children’s ability to “learn how to learn” or from task automation on novice workers acquiring relevant occupation- or task-specific skills.

The disagreements are in part about whether AI-provided information is a complement or a substitute to human learning. If the former, then expanding AI will make humans put their effort and attention in where it matters and use AI’s inputs with growing effectiveness. In the substitutes case, however, better and better AI will increasingly discourage human effort and learning — because most relevant information comes to be served to humans on a platter.

► Paper text — Introduction, part 2: the empirical evidence

Proponents of both views can find evidence to support their position. AI’s critical role in helping scientists design new proteins provides a well-known example of how these tools have already moved the dial in advancing human scientific knowledge. Using data from a leading firm in the industry, Brynjolfsson et al. (2025) have documented how AI has successfully helped customer service agents by providing them with relevant background information on the problem they are dealing with.

At the other end of the ledger, several studies show how the use of AI is negatively impacting human learning effort and sometimes even capability. Kosmyna et al. (2025) find that using ChatGPT for writing assistance reduces users’ ability to memorize and accurately record their own arguments, and remarkably, it appears to do so by changing neural connectivity in users’ brains. The authors also document that the resulting output now looks much more similar across writers, raising concerns about the erosion of individual creativity and agency. Relatedly, Gerlich (2025) finds that large language models appear to reduce the creativity of users, especially among younger users.

In fact, there is evidence that large language models have already adversely affected collective knowledge-building in a number of settings. For instance, in the context of the coding Q&A platform Stack Overflow, there is now less activity, less human engagement with questions, and arguably less generation of new knowledge as experienced coders grapple with new questions. The evidence strongly indicates that this is in response to people turning to generative AI tools to get that same help (del Rio-Chanona et al., 2024). The pattern seems to be similar for Wikipedia, with less article reading and generation in areas where ChatGPT is an effective substitute to Wikipedia (Lyu et al., 2025).

► Paper text — Introduction, part 3: the key distinction and main results

This paper is an attempt to contribute to a better theoretical understanding of how AI tools impact human cognition and knowledge. We build a dynamic model of learning and decision-making where AI inputs can be either complementary or substitutable to human effort. At the center of our approach is a distinction between two types of information: general and individual- (or context-) specific.

To perform any task, individuals require general knowledge. For example, for investment decisions one needs a basic understanding of different financial instruments. But one also needs information related to an individual’s context: what is the risk tolerance and planning horizon of the individual in question? Notably, human decision-makers often acquire both general and specific knowledge jointly — there are economies of scope in learning.

The main result of the paper is a cautionary one: a powerful agentic AI model can statically help human decision-makers, but it can dynamically harm collective knowledge building. In fact, it can lead to what we call “knowledge collapse” whereby in the long-run equilibrium all human knowledge is ultimately destroyed.

It is not surprising that, statically, individuals gain from additional information. They only reduce their effort because they already receive a fairly good recommendation from agentic AI, which is naturally a substitute to their effort. However, human effort feeds into collective knowledge, and this externality is not internalized by the agents. As they reduce their learning effort, the amount of information that either the community or AI models can aggregate starts diminishing.

We show that the negative effect is likely to dominate when agentic AI is very accurate. On the other hand, the ability of agents to learn from the general knowledge of others — either via community aggregation or more traditional AI or Internet-type tools — always improves welfare. Because agentic recommendations operate purely through information provision, these results naturally motivate regulation policies using information design. We show that limiting the effective precision of agentic recommendations via deliberate garbling can preserve learning incentives and prevent knowledge collapse.

The paper opens with a genuine disagreement among serious researchers. Both sides cite real evidence. The question is “under what conditions is AI each, and why?”

► CS translation · Two key empirical datasets

Del Rio-Chanona et al. (2024): Stack Overflow activity dropped sharply and causally after ChatGPT launch. Experienced coders have stopped answering because the questions they would answer no longer appear. Collective knowledge production declining, not just user migration.

Lyu et al. (2025): Wikipedia edits and reads declined specifically in domains where ChatGPT is an effective substitute — not globally. The selectivity rules out platform fatigue. It’s the substitution mechanism, exactly as the model predicts.

► CS translation · General vs specific knowledge

General knowledge: documentation, architecture patterns, algorithm fundamentals, canonical Stack Overflow answers. Broadly applicable, slow-changing, community-produced.

Context-specific knowledge: the fix for your particular bug, the recommendation calibrated to your codebase and constraints. AI is increasingly excellent at this.

The paper’s thesis: when AI gets very good at the second kind, people stop producing the first kind as a byproduct of their own learning — and the commons degrades.

► CS translation · How to read this diagram

The green loop is a positive feedback cycle: more knowledge X makes learning effort more rewarding (complement), effort generates community signal, signal accumulates back into X. A self-reinforcing knowledge commons — the open source / Wikipedia / Stack Overflow dynamic when it works.

The amber arrow is the disruption: agentic AI precision τA provides personalised recommendations that substitute for individual effort. This is why Wikipedia, GitHub, Stack Overflow (when they function) are so valuable: they amplify the entire virtuous cycle. And why their decline is so costly: the amplifier is failing.

The paper’s Observation 1 is precisely the formal statement that the amber arrow has negative sign (substitute: ∂²U/∂e∂τA < 0) while the X→effort link has positive sign (complement: ∂²U/∂e∂X > 0).

Section 2

Related literature

► Paper text — Related literature

Our main contribution is to provide, to the best of our knowledge, the first theoretical analysis of collective learning dynamics in the presence of AI agents and establish the possibility that AI, even as it helps static decision-making, could harm long-run collective knowledge building. Nevertheless, there are several literatures related to our work.

Most closely related to our paper are a few works studying AI’s impact on human information acquisition. Ide (2025) develops a model in which firms can decide to automate entry-level tasks but this can negatively affect long-term growth because it hampers the intergenerational transmission of tacit knowledge, typically taking place within firms via novice-expert interactions. Agarwal et al. (2025) consider human-AI collaboration in classification tasks and emphasize how AI input is a substitute to human effort. Neither of these two papers nor any other ones that we are aware of consider the effects of AI on collective knowledge acquisition when individual learning depends on the efforts of others.

Also related are a few works that discuss the impact of recent digital technologies on collective information or behavior. Acemoglu et al. (2024) and Dasaratha and He (2022) analyze the implications of social media engagement on learning. Agrawal et al. (2023), Ide and Talamàs (2025) and Cullen et al. (2025) study the effects of AI on knowledge sharing, organizational structure and productivity in firms.

The emphasis on the collective nature of human learning goes back to the early classic work by Nelson (1959) and Arrow (1962a,b). These ideas are also at the root of the recent literature on collective experimentation, for example, Bolton and Harris (1999), Keller et al. (2005) and Keller and Rady (2010), and the literature on social learning (from others’ actions), including Banerjee (1992), Bikhchandani et al. (1992) and Smith and Sørensen (2000).

We are also related to the literature on the conditions under which signals are complements or substitutes and the broader data externalities that arise within that context. See, among others, Börgers et al. (2013) and Brooks et al. (2024) on comparisons of signals, and Acemoglu et al. (2022) and Bergemann et al. (2022) on data and informational externalities.

The paper situates itself at the intersection of three literatures: AI’s impact on individual cognition, collective learning dynamics, and information economics. Its claimed novelty: the first theoretical model of how agentic AI affects collective knowledge building.

► CS translation · Why this is a new contribution

Prior models studied AI either as an automation tool (replacing tasks) or as an information source (helping individuals). None modelled the feedback loop: AI → reduced learning effort → less community knowledge → weaker future AI inputs → and the dynamic equilibrium consequences. That’s the gap this paper fills.

Section 3

The model

3.1 Environment

► Paper text — 3.1 Environment: two kinds of knowledge

Time is discrete, t = 1, 2, …. Each period t, there is a continuum of short-lived agents of total mass M > 0, indexed by i ∈ [0, M]. The population is partitioned into several islands such that general knowledge is only aggregated and shared within each island.

There are two latent states of the world, representing two types of knowledge of the society:

A common state θt evolves as a random walk: θt+1 = θt + εt, εt ~ N(0, Σ²). We interpret this common state as general knowledge that is broadly useful across the community. For example, in the context of investment decisions it may capture a basic understanding of different financial instruments and market institutions, while in the context of medical decisions it may represent basic knowledge on anatomy, bacteria, viruses, diseases and standard treatments. We model the common state as evolving over time because the frontier of general knowledge is not static: technological innovation can introduce new financial instruments and practices, and environmental or epidemiological change can lead to new diseases and therapies.

An idiosyncratic state for each agent i, independently drawn each period, θi,t ~ N(0, σ²). We interpret θi,t as context-specific knowledge that is unique to agent i in period t. For example, in the context of investment decisions it may capture the individual’s risk tolerance, planning horizon, and exposure to nonfinancial income risk; in medical decision-making it may represent patient-specific characteristics such as symptoms and physical condition.

For agent i in period t, we model her task as predicting both θt (the common state) and θi,t (her idiosyncratic state). Output depends on whether each prediction falls within a unit tolerance band. The payoff function f is weakly increasing and uses complements: ΔI = 0 (context-specific knowledge alone creates no value) and ΔX > 0 (the complementarity gain is positive).

► CS translation · The setup in ML terms

Think of it as a joint prediction problem. Each agent must simultaneously predict two targets: a shared “world state” (general knowledge) and a personal “context” (specific to them). Both predictions need to be within tolerance — it’s a joint success condition.

The world state follows a random walk — knowledge becomes stale. Last year’s best practice in security, or last decade’s understanding of machine learning, is not this year’s. Agents need to keep learning just to stay current.

“Islands” are knowledge-sharing communities. Think GitHub orgs, Stack Overflow tags, professional societies. Information produced within an island propagates to future members of that island — but not to other islands. Island size I is the key aggregation parameter.

3.2 Signals, the public good, and agentic AI

► Paper text — 3.2 Learning effort generates two signals

Before performing the tasks, each agent chooses effort ei,t ≥ 0 at cost (1/α)eαi,t where α > 1. The agent’s payoff is task output minus effort cost.

Private Learning. Agent i’s effort produces a private signal about her idiosyncratic state θi,t, with precision proportional to her effort: sHi,t ~ N(θi,t, 1/(λIei,t)).

Public Learning. An agent’s effort also generates general knowledge — information about the common state θt. Each agent is atomistic, so individual contributions to the public stock of general knowledge are infinitesimal, but the aggregation of many such contributions is still finite. Signals produced within island m are aggregated into a public social signal sCm,t ~ N(θt, 1/(λGEm,t)), where Em,t is aggregate effort on island m. This public signal is shared with all future cohorts on island m.

Because each individual’s contribution is infinitesimal, the public-learning component of effort does not generate private incentives, so that the learning externality is fully uninternalized by the agents. For example, when a software engineer diagnoses a rare production bug, the main private payoff comes from restoring their own system (a highly context-specific fix), whereas turning the insight into a clear, maintained public write-up (e.g., on Stack Overflow) would benefit many future developers, but yields only a small private return.

► Paper text — 3.2 Agentic AI as an additional context-specific signal

We now introduce agentic AI. We model agentic AI as an information technology that provides individual, context-specific information. For example, such an agentic system might provide personalized medical recommendations or investment suggestions tailored to the user. Accordingly, we model agentic AI as providing each agent a signal about her idiosyncratic state: sAi,t ~ N(θi,t, 1/τA), where τA ≥ 0 captures the accuracy (precision) of agentic AI.

In the baseline model, agentic AI neither provides information about the common state directly nor improves the technology by which dispersed human efforts are pooled into the public knowledge. This assumption is an approximation to a world in which general knowledge is already well aggregated via in-person social learning and other pre-AI tools. In the medical example, this would be aligned with the case in which agentic AI does not invent new treatments or discover new diseases, and does not inform specialists about important general knowledge.

The within-period timing is as follows: (i) each agent observes the public history on their island and chooses effort; (ii) each agent observes their private signal; (iii) each agent receives the agentic AI recommendation; (iv) each agent forms predictions and receives utility; (v) the community aggregates general-knowledge signals within each island.

► CS translation · Three information sources, one Bayesian fusion

Each agent has three information sources: (1) the community’s accumulated general knowledge — a prior built from past cohorts’ aggregated learning; (2) their own private learning effort — produces a noisy signal about their specific context; (3) agentic AI — a separate noisy signal about their specific context, precision τA.

Sources (2) and (3) both target the same thing — the agent’s specific context. They’re substitutes. Adding more of one reduces the marginal value of the other. Source (1) targets something different — general knowledge — so it’s a complement, not a substitute, to the effort-produced private signal.

Why agentic specifically, not just AI in general? Tier 1 — search engines, encyclopedic LLM Q&A: faster access to existing general knowledge. Complement. Learning incentives intact. Tier 2 — LLMs synthesising across sources: helps aggregate general knowledge. Also broadly positive. Tier 3 — agentic AI providing your personal answer: directly substitutes for private-signal learning. The dangerous tier, and the current development direction.

3.3–3.4 Equilibrium and the substitutes/complements result

► Paper text — 3.3–3.4 Utility structure and Observation 1

Under Assumption 1 (ΔI = 0, ΔX > 0), agent i’s expected period-t utility can be written as:

Ui,t = f(0,0) + G(Xt) · ΔG + G(Xt)G(Yi,t) · ΔX − (1/α)eαi,t

where Xt is the public precision (stock of general knowledge), Yi,t = σ−2 + λIei,t + τA is the agent’s idiosyncratic precision, and G(τ) = Pr(|Z| ≤ 1) for Z ~ N(0, τ−1) is the probability of a successful prediction.

Observation 1. Public precision Xt complements human effort, while agentic-AI precision τA substitutes for human effort: ∂²U/∂e∂X > 0, ∂²U/∂e∂τA < 0.

General knowledge (Xt) is a complement to human effort. An agent’s private return to effort comes entirely through the complementarity term G(Xt)G(Yi,t) · ΔX. Learning effort raises Yi,t, and its marginal benefit is proportional to G(Xt): when the stock of general knowledge is higher, improving context-specific precision is more valuable because the two inputs are complements in task production.

On the other hand, agentic-AI precision enters only through Yi,t, directly improving context-specific precision. Because the probability of success G(Y) exhibits diminishing returns in precision, higher τA reduces the marginal gain from additional human effort. Thus, better agentic recommendations crowd out learning effort by making incremental self-acquired precision less valuable at the margin.

Observation 2. The best-response effort e(X, τA) is increasing in X and decreasing in τA, strictly so for all X > 0.

Observation 1
The fundamental asymmetry — the whole paper in one result

General knowledge complements effort. When community knowledge X is high, each unit of personal learning effort is worth more. More community knowledge → more incentive to learn. Positive feedback.

AI precision substitutes for effort. When AI gives accurate personalised recommendations, the return to developing your own judgment falls. Higher τA → lower marginal value of learning effort. Negative feedback on knowledge production.

Consequence: Higher AI precision → individuals reduce effort → less general knowledge contributed → lower community stock → lower marginal return to effort → effort falls further. At every step individuals behave rationally. The failure is purely collective.

3.5–3.6 Dynamic equilibrium and steady states

► Paper text — 3.5 Proposition 1: the dynamic equilibrium

Fix a period t. Taking the current public precision Xt and agentic-AI precision τA as given, agent i chooses effort to maximize expected utility. The first-order condition is:

ΔXG(Xt) · λIg(σ−2 + λIei,t + τA) − eα−1i,t = 0

The marginal benefit is decreasing in effort (g(·) is decreasing), while the marginal cost is strictly increasing. Consequently, utility is strictly concave in effort and admits a unique and finite maximizer.

Proposition 1 (Dynamic equilibrium). There exists a unique symmetric Perfect Bayesian equilibrium path {Xt, et} satisfying:

et = e(Xt, τA)    Xt+1 = [Σ² + (Xt + λGIet)−1]−1

Substituting the best response into the law of motion yields a one-dimensional state transition map Xt+1 = F(Xt). The dynamic equilibrium reduces to iterating F on Xt, with effort et adjusting endogenously via the best response.

Two forces shape the evolution of general knowledge. First, knowledge accumulation: higher current effort et raises next period’s public precision. Second, knowledge depreciation: because the common state follows a random walk, what was learned in the past becomes less relevant, captured by the innovation variance Σ².

► Paper text — 3.6 Steady states and the knowledge-collapse steady state

A steady-state equilibrium is a scalar X̄ satisfying X̄ = F(X̄).

An important type of steady state is the degenerate one where (X̄, ē, Ȳ) = (0, 0, σ−2 + τA). This corresponds to a situation in which the community accumulates essentially no usable general knowledge in the long run. Even though agentic AI may still deliver relevant individual- or context-specific information, this is not socially useful because general knowledge that is an essential input disappears and makes the context-specific information also useless. This is the knowledge-collapse steady state.

Lemma 1. The state transition map F is continuous and strictly increasing on R≥0, with F(0) = 0 and F(+∞) = Σ−2.

The monotonicity of F stems from the fundamental complementarity between general knowledge and returns to learning effort: a higher inherited stock of general knowledge X raises current effort e(X, τA), which strengthens the public signal and increases next period’s public precision.

Proposition 2. The function F is pointwise increasing in I and pointwise decreasing in τA, strictly so for all X > 0.

► CS translation · Fixed points and Kalman updates

The law of motion Xt+1 = F(Xt) is a Kalman-like update: community knowledge grows from this period’s human learning, then is diluted by world-state drift (random walk noise Σ²). Steady states are fixed points of F — where knowledge exactly self-replenishes.

The knowledge-collapse state X̄ = 0 is always a fixed point: if nobody knows anything, nobody learns, nobody contributes, nobody knows anything next period. The interesting question is whether other stable fixed points exist — and whether the zero fixed point is stable or unstable.

3.7–3.8 The two regimes

► Paper text — 3.7 Unique steady state regime (α−1 > ¼)

We first provide a more detailed analysis of the regime in which effort costs are sufficiently convex, meaning that α − 1 > 1/4 (equivalently ε := 1/(α−1) < 4), so that optimal effort is not “too elastic.”

Proposition 3. Suppose α − 1 > 1/4. Then there exist exactly two steady-state equilibria, 0 = X̄ℓ < X̄h < +∞, where X̄h is locally stable and X̄ℓ is locally unstable. Moreover, for any X1 > 0, Xt → X̄h.

Proposition 3 describes a “robust learning regime”: although X = 0 is always a steady state, it is a knife-edge outcome: any strictly positive stock of general knowledge, no matter how small, triggers enough learning effort to more than offset depreciation, and the economy converges to the unique high-knowledge steady state X̄h. There is no coordination failure.

Proposition 4. Suppose α − 1 > 1/4. (i) As agentic AI precision τA increases: X̄h ↓, ēh ↓, Ȳh ↑. (ii) As the aggregation scale I increases: X̄h ↑, ēh ↑, Ȳh ↑.

Part (i): even in the robust-learning regime, more accurate agentic AI erodes collective knowledge. A higher τA reduces learning incentives, lowering the flow of new human-generated information. At the same time, idiosyncratic precision Ȳh rises mechanically with τA. Thus, agentic AI improves the context-specific information available at steady state while reducing the general-knowledge stock that sustains complementary human learning.

Part (ii): larger aggregation capacity strengthens the intertemporal complementarity across cohorts, making the knowledge stock more resilient and pushing the economy toward a higher-knowledge steady state.

► Paper text — 3.8 Multiple steady states and knowledge collapse (α−1 < ¼)

For our purposes, the more interesting part of the parameter space is where multiple steady states can arise: α − 1 < 1/4 (equivalently ε > 4), in which case effort is highly elastic and the zero-knowledge steady state X̄ = 0 is locally stable. This local stability opens the door to multiple stable long-run outcomes.

Proposition 5. Suppose α − 1 < 1/4. There exists threshold τcA ≥ 0, such that:

(i) If τA < τcA, there exist three steady-state equilibria 0 = X̄ℓ < X̄m < X̄h < +∞, where X̄ℓ and X̄h are locally stable, whereas X̄m is locally unstable. The unstable point X̄m partitions the state space: X1 < X̄m ⇒ Xt → 0; X1 > X̄m ⇒ Xt → X̄h.

(ii) If τA > τcA, then the unique steady-state equilibrium is the knowledge-collapse one, X̄ℓ = 0, and this steady state is globally stable: for all X1 ≥ 0, Xt → X̄ℓ.

Another interesting feature of Proposition 5 is that the long-run outcome can change discontinuously with τA. As τA ↑ τcA from below, the two positive fixed points X̄m and X̄h move toward each other and eventually collide; at τcA the positive steady states disappear, after which the only remaining attractor is X̄ℓ = 0. Consequently, the long-run stock of general knowledge can exhibit a discontinuous drop as τA crosses τcA.

Propositions 6 & 7. The complete-collapse threshold τcA is strictly increasing in aggregation capacity I and in complementarity ΔX. The basin-of-attraction threshold X̄m is increasing in τA and decreasing in I and ΔX.

Lemma 2. (i) If α−1 > 1/4, then F(X) > X in a neighbourhood of 0, so the knowledge-collapse steady state is locally unstable. (ii) If α−1 < 1/4, then F(X) < X in a neighbourhood of 0, so the knowledge-collapse steady state is locally stable.

Whether the zero steady state is locally stable comes down to comparing the effort needed to keep a tiny amount of public knowledge alive with the effort agents actually choose in equilibrium. When α − 1 < 1/4, equilibrium effort falls faster than the maintenance level, and any small positive X drifts back to zero.

Proposition 5
Knowledge collapse — the central result
  • If τA < τᶜA: three equilibria. Outcome depends on starting point. Current knowledge above X̄m → converges to high state. Below X̄m → spirals to zero.
  • If τA exceeds τᶜA (complete collapse): the high-knowledge equilibrium disappears entirely. Economy converges to zero general knowledge from any initial condition.

The transition is discontinuous — a small increase in AI precision can cause a large, irreversible drop in long-run general knowledge. Larger aggregation capacity I raises τᶜA: stronger communities tolerate more accurate AI before collapse.

► CS translation · Saddle-node bifurcation, basins of attraction

Fully standard 1D discrete dynamical systems. The map F(X) crosses the 45° line at fixed points. Stable intersections (slope < 1) are attractors; unstable (slope > 1) are boundaries. The three-equilibrium case: two stable attractors separated by unstable threshold X̄m.

Complete collapse is a saddle-node bifurcation: as τA increases, the two positive fixed points X̄m and X̄h collide and annihilate. After the bifurcation only the zero attractor remains. The system looks fine — until it suddenly doesn’t. Classic tipping-point structure.

Section 4

Welfare

► Paper text — 4.1 Steady-state expected utility

We take the relevant welfare object to be the steady-state expected utility of a representative cohort. For any steady state (X̄, ē, Ȳ), define steady-state expected utility as:

Ū := G(X̄) · ΔG + G(X̄)G(Ȳ) · ΔX − (1/α)ēα

Under Assumption 1 (ΔI = 0), the knowledge-collapse steady state (X̄, ē) = 0 yields Ū = 0.

The realized long-run welfare depends on whether the equilibrium dynamics admit a unique stable steady state or exhibit multiplicity and path dependence. In the unique steady state regime (α−1 > 1/4), long-run welfare is uniquely pinned down. In the multiple steady states regime (α−1 < 1/4), equilibrium dynamics can display coordination failure: if τA ≥ τcA, complete collapse occurs and long-run welfare is 0. If τA < τcA, long-run welfare depends on the initial stock X1.

► Paper text — 4.2 & 4.3 The welfare effects of aggregation and agentic AI

Proposition 9. Whenever the high-knowledge steady state exists, Ū+ is strictly increasing in I.

Higher I enables better aggregation of general knowledge and raises welfare through two distinct channels: (i) a level effect — a larger I increases the informativeness of the public signal generated by any given per-capita effort, shifting F upward and raising X̄h; (ii) a selection effect — when α−1 < 1/4, a larger I also lowers X̄m, expanding the basin of attraction of the high-knowledge steady state.

The welfare effects of a stronger agentic AI (higher τA) are inherently ambiguous:

∂Ū+/∂τA = g(Ȳh) · G(X̄h)ΔX [Direct Effect, ≥ 0] + ∂G(X̄h)/∂τA · (ΔG + G(Ȳh)ΔX) [Indirect Effect, ≤ 0]

The direct effect is the mechanical gain from more accurate individualized recommendations. The indirect effect reflects dynamic crowd-out: higher τA reduces equilibrium effort, weakening the flow of human-generated information and lowering X̄h.

The ratio IE/DE is always increasing in τA and strictly above one when τA is sufficiently large. Two economic forces push the ratio upward: (1) the direct benefit DE exhibits diminishing returns — once AI is already fairly accurate, further increases barely change outcomes; (2) the indirect loss IE becomes larger when X̄h is low — losing general knowledge gets more costly as there is less of it.

► Paper text — 4.3 Propositions 10 & 11: non-monotone welfare

Proposition 10. Suppose α − 1 > 1/4 and Assumption 2 holds. There exists a finite threshold τ*A satisfying 0 ≤ τ*A < +∞, such that: Ū+ is strictly increasing in τA on (0, τ*A); strictly decreasing on (τ*A, +∞); and Ū+ → 0 as τA → +∞.

Proposition 11. Suppose α − 1 < 1/4 and Assumption 2 holds. There exists a finite threshold τ*A satisfying 0 ≤ τ*A < τcA, such that: Ū+ is strictly increasing on (0, τ*A); strictly decreasing on (τ*A, τcA); and Ū+ = 0 for τA > τcA.

Relative to the unique steady state regime, the welfare consequences are more stark here because a sufficiently accurate AI can induce a discontinuous regime change: once τA crosses τcA, the high-knowledge steady state disappears and long-term knowledge collapse becomes inevitable. Moreover, even for τA < τcA, realized welfare need not track Ū+ because the economy may fail to coordinate on the high-knowledge basin of attraction, and larger τA can increase the likelihood of coordination failure.

► Paper text — 4.4 Proposition 12: the scaling law

Proposition 12. Suppose Assumption 2 holds. As the aggregation capacity I goes to infinity (with all other parameters fixed):

limI→+∞ τcA / log I = 2(α − 1),   and   limI→+∞ τ*A / log I = 2(α − 1)/α.

Proposition 12 delivers a sharp “scaling law”-type result. Both the collapse threshold τcA and the welfare-maximizing precision τ*A grow only logarithmically in I, reflecting strong diminishing returns to aggregation. The economic reason is that, as τA increases, the marginal value of human effort in producing idiosyncratic precision falls very quickly, so equilibrium effort declines sharply. Consequently, sustaining a given public-knowledge level requires the aggregation scale to grow very fast.

Finally, this asymptotic result implies that even as aggregation becomes arbitrarily powerful, the welfare-maximizing accuracy remains a fixed fraction away from the collapse boundary: τ*A/τcA → 1/α. Another important conclusion is therefore that the planner optimally chooses to stay bounded away from the tipping point.

Propositions 9, 10, 11, 12
Non-monotone welfare and the log ceiling
  • Welfare is strictly increasing in aggregation capacity I — always, no exceptions.
  • Welfare is non-monotone in AI precision τA. Interior welfare-maximising τ*A exists. Above it: more accurate AI reduces welfare.
  • Both thresholds grow as O(log I) in aggregation capacity — logarithmically. To double the collapse threshold, you need to roughly square the aggregation capacity.
  • The welfare optimum stays at fraction 1/α below the collapse boundary, regardless of how large I gets.

► CS translation · Why the welfare curve must eventually turn down

The direct benefit of precision is bounded — a 99% accurate AI made 99.9% accurate barely moves outcomes. The indirect damage is unbounded in the limit — as community knowledge stock approaches zero, the cost of losing it grows without bound. The log scaling is sobering. If AI capability doubles every year or two, you need to square your knowledge-sharing infrastructure to keep up. That’s not a feasible trajectory. Precision regulation — directly limiting AI accuracy — is the only lever that addresses the substitution effect at source.

4.5 Information design / regulation

► Paper text — 4.5 Proposition 13: the optimal garbling policy

Motivated by the possible negative effects of agentic AI and the potential for knowledge collapse, we discuss regulation policies using information design.

Formally, we model regulation as garbling of the agentic recommendation by additive independent Gaussian noise. An information design policy can be represented by the sequence κ = {κt}t≥1, where κt is the precision of the noise added in period t. Garbling is equivalent to replacing the raw agentic precision τA by an effective precision τ(t)A ∈ [0, τA].

We evaluate policies by long-run average welfare: U(κ) := lim infT→∞ (1/T)∑t=1T Ut(κ).

Proposition 13. Suppose that α − 1 < 1/4, and fix initial public precision X1 > X̄m(0). Define τ* := min{τA, τ*A} and pick κ* such that τA(κ*) = τ*. Then the two-phase policy

κ2pht = 0 for t < M*,  κ* for t ≥ M*

satisfies U(κ2ph) = Ū+(τ*) and is optimal among all eventually constant policies.

The economic logic: Phase 1 (fully suppress AI, κt = 0) maximises equilibrium effort, accelerating the rebuilding of public general knowledge and pushing the state into the basin of attraction of the high-knowledge steady state under the eventual cap. Phase 2 (cap at τ*) implements a permanent precision cap at the welfare-maximising level. If τA ≤ τ*A, then τ* = τA and Phase 2 corresponds to no garbling; if τA > τ*A, Phase 2 implements a strict precision cap.

Intuitively, Phase 1 is dealing with the problem of path-dependence: it ensures that the economy escapes the basin of attraction of the knowledge-collapse steady state. Phase 2 then sets an optimal long-run cap.

Proposition 13
The optimal two-phase policy
  • Phase 1 — Suppression: fully block AI recommendations for finite period M*. Rebuilds the community knowledge commons until it clears the basin-of-attraction threshold X̄m(τ*).
  • Phase 2 — Optimal cap: permanently limit effective AI precision to τ* = min(τA, τ*A). Allows AI to deliver its welfare-improving benefit without triggering the crowd-out spiral.

If current knowledge already exceeds the Phase 2 threshold, Phase 1 is unnecessary. Phase 1 duration M* is finite and explicitly characterised.

► CS translation · Circuit breaker + rate limiter

Phase 1 is a circuit breaker — cut the problematic input to allow system recovery. Phase 2 is a rate limiter — permanent cap preventing the system from exceeding a safe operating threshold. Standard resilience engineering pattern. The difference: the trigger here isn’t a fault — it’s the normal, rational behaviour of individually-optimising agents producing catastrophic collective externalities.

Real-world implementation: mandating uncertainty quantification in AI outputs (forces human judgement); requiring human-in-the-loop above confidence thresholds; capping specificity of AI recommendations in high-stakes domains; mandatory “think before you ask AI” periods in education (Phase 1 equivalent).

Section 5

Extensions

5.1 What if AI also improves knowledge aggregation?

► Paper text — 5.1 Community versus AI aggregation (Proposition 14)

In the baseline model, agentic AI only affects context-specific information but does not change the economy’s stock of general knowledge directly. This subsection considers an extension in which the same AI technology simultaneously (i) provides agent-specific recommendations and (ii) improves the stock of general knowledge, replacing the fixed island size I by an AI-dependent effective aggregation scale I(τA) := I0 + exp(ητA), where η ≥ 0 governs how strongly improvements in agentic capability translate into better aggregation.

Unlike the baseline model, τA now bundles together two opposing forces: a substitution (agentic) channel and a complementarity (aggregation) channel. Which force dominates depends on η.

Proposition 14. Suppose that the AI-driven aggregation does not grow too quickly in that η < ηcrit := 1/[2(α−1)]. Then the high-knowledge steady state (when it exists) satisfies X̄h(τA) ↓ 0 as τA → ∞. As a result, the welfare-maximizing level of AI is finite.

Proposition 14 highlights that allowing AI to simultaneously improve the stock of general knowledge does not change our main conclusions as long as this effect is not too pronounced.

► CS translation · The burden shifts to the optimist

To escape the collapse result, you need to show that AI’s aggregation benefits grow faster than sub-exponentially in AI capability — a strong, empirically uncertain claim. “AI helps with knowledge synthesis” is not sufficient. Given current evidence on declining open knowledge platforms, the optimistic defence looks hard to sustain.

5.2 Synthetic data

► Paper text — 5.2 Synthetic data (Proposition 15)

The baseline model assumes that new general knowledge is generated only by human effort. By contrast, in rule-governed or mechanically verifiable environments (such as reinforcement learning applied to games, algorithmic search, or formal proof checking), AI can generate “synthetic data” that is paired with an objective correctness signal and can therefore create real novelty.

We model this by supposing that in each period an additional exogenous signal about the common state is produced with precision τsyn ≥ 0. If τsyn > 0, then Fsyn(0) > 0, so the degenerate “zero-knowledge” steady state is no longer a fixed point — synthetic data raises the floor of the collapse state from zero to some positive value.

Proposition 15. Suppose 0 < τsyn < +∞. The least and greatest steady states X̄*ℓ and X̄*h are strictly positive. All four quantities X̄*h, ē*h, X̄*ℓ, ē*ℓ are strictly increasing in I and τsyn, and strictly decreasing in τA.

Our main results generalize to this environment, provided that synthetic data cannot fully replace human-generated knowledge.

5.3 Separability of effort

► Paper text — 5.3 Imperfect separability (Proposition 16)

In the baseline model, a unit of learning effort jointly produces context-specific knowledge and a “thin” contribution to general knowledge. Here we allow agents to partially decouple these two outputs, parameterising the public-learning contribution of effort as λGeβi,t where β ∈ [0, +∞). The limiting case β = 0 corresponds to perfect separability: changes in effort do not alter the public-learning input at all.

Proposition 16. For any β > 0 (not perfectly separable), all results in the baseline model continue to apply, except with the stability condition α − 1 < 1/4 replaced by α − 1 < β/4, and with the asymptotic results in Proposition 12 replaced by:

limI→∞ τcA / log I = 2(α−1)/β   and   limI→∞ τ*A / log I = 2(α−1)/(α−1+β).

The intuition: if β is large, eβ declines very rapidly as e ↓ 0, strengthening the self-reinforcing logic behind collapse. If β is small, a small amount of human effort will be sufficient to produce significant improvements in general knowledge, making knowledge collapse less likely. Only perfect separability (β = 0) eliminates the collapse dynamic.

Section 6

Conclusion

► Paper text — Conclusion

In this paper, we introduced a simple framework to study the implications of new generative AI technologies that promise to provide context-specific information and recommendations to human decision-makers. Our framework is based on three core ideas:

  1. Good human decisions combine general knowledge with context-specific information.
  2. Human effort directed at improving cognition generates both types of information, with the primary private return coming from context-specific information.
  3. Individual contributions to general knowledge create externalities on others who build on this general knowledge.

These three observations together imply that the main motive for individual effort is often the acquisition of context-specific information, while the general knowledge an individual generates is primarily an externality. Consequently, better general knowledge in society is a complement to human learning effort, while better context-specific recommendations are substitutes. Because generative AI promises to provide this kind of context-specific information, it can be such a substitute, and reduce human effort. But then as lower human effort reduces general knowledge-building, generative AI (especially agentic AI) can dynamically push a society towards lower effective information and even lead to a knowledge-collapse steady state.

Our analysis is purely theoretical and shows that such a framework is tractable and yields a number of new and intuitive comparative statics. It also clarifies the conditions under which a knowledge-collapse steady state emerges and what determines how large its basin of attraction is.

There are several interesting areas for future research, including on incorporating synthetic data and other new AI capabilities, and exploring whether they can break the strong substitution between context-specific AI recommendations and human effort. Our framework also provides guidelines on different types of effects that need to be measured empirically to evaluate the overall welfare impacts of new AI advances.

Summary
Three things to carry away
  • Welfare is non-monotone in AI accuracy. An interior optimal precision exists, strictly below the maximum achievable.
  • Aggregation infrastructure always helps. No non-monotonicity — investment in knowledge-sharing is unambiguously good. But protection scales only O(log I) against exponential AI capability growth.
  • Precision caps are the right regulatory tool. The optimal policy garbles recommendations to preserve learning incentives. Phase 1 rebuilds the commons; Phase 2 maintains it.
► CS translation · Which domains are most at risk?

Risk depends on effort elasticity α. Domains where professional standards enforce minimum learning — medicine, law, academia — have high resistance (low ε). Domains with elastic effort and fast AI adoption — software development, content creation, retail financial advice — are closer to the dangerous regime (high ε).

The model predicts junior professionals growing up with agentic AI will have significantly lower general domain knowledge than previous generations — not because they’re less capable, but because the incentive structure has changed.

Appendix

Formal proof sketches

Brief outlines. Full proofs in the original PDF.

► Lemma 1 — State transition map properties
Shows F is continuous, strictly increasing on R≥0 with F(0)=0 and F(+∞)=Σ⁻². Best-response effort e(X,τA) is continuous and strictly increasing in X via the implicit function theorem. F inherits monotonicity.
► Proposition 1 — Equilibrium existence and uniqueness
Expected utility is strictly concave in effort (diminishing marginal benefit, convex cost), so best response e(Xt,τA) is unique. By symmetry all agents on an island choose identically. The Kalman recursion Xt+1 = F(Xt) pins down a unique symmetric equilibrium path.
► Propositions 3 & 5 — Steady-state characterisation
The “effort-first” trick rewrites F(X̄)=X̄ in terms of steady-state effort ē. Yields D(e)=0 where log(MB(x)) is strictly concave in x=log(e) while log(MC(x))=(α−1)x is linear. When α−1>¼: exactly one positive root. When α−1<¼: zero or two positive roots, with transition at τᶜA where roots coalesce in a saddle-node bifurcation.
► Lemma 2 — Stability of the zero steady state
Near X=0, maintaining a small stock requires effort of order Θ(X²). Equilibrium effort near zero is Θ(X^{1/(2(α−1))}). When α−1>¼: exponent<2, effort decays slower than maintenance — zero is locally unstable. When α−1<¼: exponent>2, effort decays faster — zero is locally stable.
► Propositions 9–11 — Welfare
Welfare comparative statics use the Envelope Theorem. Key quantity: ratio IE/DE (indirect to direct effect of τA). Assumption 2 rules out double sign changes. IE/DE is strictly increasing in τA. Threshold τ*A is where IE/DE=1. Welfare is single-peaked.
► Proposition 12 — Scaling laws
As I→∞, aggregate effort at the collapse tangency satisfies ĒI→E* (unique root of R(E*)=α−1). Gaussian tail approximation gives τᶜA ~ 2(α−1)·log I. Welfare-optimal τ*A ~ 2(α−1)/α·log I. Ratio τ*A/τᶜA → 1/α, always strictly less than 1.
► Proposition 13 — Optimal information design
For any eventually-constant policy, long-run welfare is 0 or Ū⁺(τ̄). Under full suppression (Phase 1), state converges to X̄h(0) > X̄m(τ*) in finite time M*. Optimality: any eventually-constant policy achieves at most maxτ Ū⁺(τ) = Ū⁺(τ*), achieved exactly by the two-phase policy.
► Propositions 14–16 — Extensions
Prop 14 (AI improves aggregation, η<ηcrit): exponential-decay upper bound on effort implies I(τA)·ēh→0 as τA→∞. Prop 15 (synthetic data): Tarski’s fixed-point theorem gives existence of extremal steady states; τsyn>0 makes F(0)>0 so least fixed point is strictly positive. Prop 16 (separable effort): same local stability argument with 1/4→β/4 throughout.
References

Cited works — annotated

Key papers cited in this work, with brief summaries of what they contribute and why they appear here.

► Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond (2025) — Generative AI at work · QJE 140(2)

Randomised evaluation at a large US customer service firm. Agents given real-time AI suggestions based on each customer issue. Results: average handle time fell, customer satisfaction rose, and gains were largest for less-experienced workers. Expert agents gained less. Cited as the primary evidence AI can complement human effort — specifically because the AI provided general knowledge (best-practice responses) rather than a fully personalised recommendation replacing judgment. The most important piece of evidence on the optimist side.

► Kosmyna et al. (2025) — Your brain on ChatGPT · arXiv:2506.08872

EEG study. Participants who wrote essays with ChatGPT showed reduced engagement in memory and language-processing brain regions. Follow-up: less able to recall and reconstruct their own arguments. Alarming additional finding: neural connectivity patterns changed measurably. And: output looked more similar across writers. Cited as direct neurological evidence that AI assistance reduces the human cognitive effort that produces the “private signal” in the model — exactly the substitution mechanism the paper formalises.

► Del Rio-Chanona, Laurentsyeva & Wachs (2024) — LLMs reduce knowledge sharing on Q&A platforms · PNAS Nexus 3(9)

Causal analysis of Stack Overflow using ChatGPT’s release as a natural experiment. Activity dropped sharply and the drop was concentrated in question categories where ChatGPT performs best. Critically: experienced developers who write high-quality canonical answers have largely stopped contributing — not because they left, but because the questions they would answer no longer appear. Directly motivates the claim that agentic AI is already eroding community knowledge production.

► Lyu et al. (2025) — Wikipedia contributions in the wake of ChatGPT · WWW Companion

Wikipedia editing behaviour before and after ChatGPT launch. Editing declined selectively — specifically in topic areas where ChatGPT provides good answers — while areas where AI is weak saw no decline. The selectivity rules out platform fatigue and supports the substitution mechanism: when AI can answer a question, humans stop contributing to the commons that answers it.

► Shumailov et al. (2024) — AI models collapse when trained on recursively generated data · Nature 631

Models trained on AI-generated text suffer systematic degradation — “model collapse.” Output quality and diversity decline across successive generations of AI-trained-on-AI-output. Cited in the synthetic data extension (Section 5.2): even if AI generates knowledge synthetically, it cannot replace human-generated data indefinitely. Open-world settings lack the verifiable ground truth that makes synthetic self-play work (as in AlphaGo).

► Acemoglu & Restrepo (2018, 2019) — Automation and labor · AER / JEP

The foundational task-based automation model. Technology either displaces workers from existing tasks or creates new tasks. The 2018 AER paper shows automation can reduce labour’s share of income even while raising GDP. Background to this paper: the knowledge-collapse model extends the automation framework by adding collective knowledge dynamics, showing harm from AI goes beyond task displacement to eroding the knowledge infrastructure that makes human labour productive.

► Gerlich (2025) — AI tools in society: cognitive offloading and critical thinking · Societies 15(1)

Survey and experimental study. Heavy LLM use correlates with reduced critical thinking and creativity, stronger effects among younger users. Interpreted as “cognitive offloading” — delegating thinking to AI reduces the mental effort that builds cognitive capacity. Cited alongside Kosmyna et al. as evidence the substitution effect is real and measurable.

► Ide (2025) — Automation and intergenerational transmission of knowledge · arXiv:2507.16078

Closely related theory paper. Models firms deciding whether to automate entry-level tasks. Finding: automating entry-level work harms long-run growth because it severs the novice-expert mentoring relationship through which tacit knowledge is transmitted across generations. Similar mechanism to this paper but at the firm level, not community level. Cited as the most closely related prior theoretical work.

► Agarwal, Moehring & Wolitzky (2025) — Designing human-AI collaboration · NBER WP

Mechanism design approach to human-AI collaboration in classification tasks. Derives the optimal combination of AI recommendations with human effort, showing the optimal scheme depends on comparative advantage. Cited as the most closely related mechanism-design work; differs from this paper in studying static collaboration rather than dynamic collective knowledge effects.

► Arrow (1962) — The economic implications of learning by doing · Review of Economic Studies 29(3)

The classic paper on endogenous productivity growth through experience. Workers become more productive the more they do a task — knowledge accumulates as an externality from production. Intellectual ancestor of the model’s public-goods learning structure: the idea that individual productive activity generates community knowledge as a byproduct is Arrow 1962.

► Bolton & Harris (1999) — Strategic experimentation · Econometrica 67(2)

Agents choose whether to experiment (costly but informative) or free-ride on others’ experiments. In equilibrium, there is too little experimentation relative to the social optimum. The canonical reference for under-provision of public knowledge. This paper’s model is in this lineage: human learning effort is the “experimentation,” the public signal is the free-riding externality.

► Acemoglu, Ozdaglar & Siderius (2024) — A model of online misinformation · Review of Economic Studies 91(6)

From two of this paper’s authors. Models how social media engagement dynamics amplify misinformation. Relevant here as a prior paper in the same research programme: collective information systems (social media, knowledge platforms) can have dynamics that are individually rational but collectively pathological. This paper applies similar techniques to AI rather than social media.

► Cullen, Li & Li (2025) — Labor as capital: AI and the ownership of expertise · unpublished

Analyses what happens when AI can replicate expert knowledge — the expertise that workers have accumulated is effectively expropriated. AI firms can substitute AI outputs for human expertise without compensating the humans whose knowledge trained the AI. Cited as a related study of AI’s effect on the value of human knowledge and organisational structure.

► Silver et al. (2017) — Mastering Go without human knowledge · Nature 550

AlphaGo Zero: a system that learned Go from scratch by self-play, without human game records, and surpassed all previous versions. Cited in the synthetic data extension as the canonical example of a domain where AI-generated synthetic data works well — because Go is a closed, fully verifiable world with an objective correctness signal. Contrasted with open-world knowledge generation where such verification is impossible.
